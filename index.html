About

This site is a repository for my notes from Critical Theory I, a course taught in the CFA at Carnegie Mellon University. I've organized the notes relatively chronologicaly and by topic, starting with the Renaissance all the way to the dawn of Modernism with Realism. The summaries include key terms, concepts, and artists, which can all be highlighted by clicking on the links at the top of each topic's page. Additionally, I've included images that are useful to know and relate to the core ideas in the notes. All works included on this site are open-source; you can find their sources by clicking on them.

A New Vision: The Renaissance

Concepts and Terms- Humanism, Mathematical Perspective, Patronism, Sacra conversazione

Artists and Critics- Giotto, Brunelleschi, Masaccio, Fra Angelico, Piero della Francesca

Readings- “The Eye… Like a God of Human Parts”

Introduction

The period known as the Renaissance is often split into many sub-categories, often determined by a timeframe or by geographical location of the movement; the Early, Mid, Late, Northern Renaissance and many other distinctions are common. However, when considering the theory of the Renaissance, it is perhaps more important to observe it as a singular occurrence in history, and understand the changes in philosophy that made it distinct and unique.

Humanism

Humanism was a newfound appreciation and study of antiquity which rejected the medieval approach to understanding the world and people’s place in it. It was a large cultural movement, spurred on by artists, architects, writers, and scholars during the Renaissance.

The first influences of a Humanist perspective can be seen in 1300’s architecture. The soaring spires and windows of the Gothic cathedrals were replaced by squat, human scaled churches. The circle became an important part of their design, as its soft and welcoming shape made the churches more planted at a size that accommodated people particularly well. Design elements such as circular columns and semi-circular arches were adopted from Greek and Roman classical architecture and were incorporated into new churches.

Vision

Perhaps most central change that was produced by the Renaissance was a completely new understanding of sight and vision which stemmed from nature. Leone Battista Alberti, an author, artist, and scholar wrote:

“The fundamental principle will be that all steps of learning should be sought from Nature; the means of perfecting our art will be found in diligence, study, and application.”

Nature provided artists with a new way to understand the world, beyond simply theological bounds. Vision, and visual representation, became a highly valued manner of explanation. As such, images became equally important means of communicating ideas as literary sources, and as their value grew so too did that of artists.

The first product of the new observation of the world was mathematical perspective. The architect Brunelleschi is particularly well known for his early experiments into perspective, one of which, the Experiment of the Mirror (1413) is widely considered the most important. In it, Brunelleschi mounted an extremely realistic painting of a building made using mathematical one point perspective on a handle, with a hole in the middle. The viewer then held a mirror up to the painting, faced the painting away from them, and looked through the hole to see the front of the painting. When removing the mirror, they would see no difference between the painting and the real building in front of them.

Through mathematical one point perspective, new styles of artwork that embraced realism emerged, a departure from the cartoonish medieval and byzantine paintings, drawings, and frescos. The work of Giotto, for instance, exemplifies this shift. Although he worked before the discovery of one point perspective, a his frescos show a real difference in style and technical ability. Patronism

By the mid 1400’s, a new trend began to emerge where artists would include their patrons in their paintings and frescos. In Holy Trinity, Masaccio includes his patrons alongside his depiction of the Holy Trinity, Mary, and St. John.

This depiction became known as Sacra conversazione (holy/sacred conversation) and often depicted Mary and Jesus surrounded by saints and patrons.

Although this practice satisfied the patrons, who were often wealthy bankers and looking for salvation, it became an issue for many religious hardliners, who felt that such an act was unacceptable. Donor portraits, among many other reasons, would eventually lead to the Reformation, discussed in the next section.

Characteristics of Artworks

1350-1450

  • Stiff, unrealistic representations
  • Mainly religious imagery
  • Lots of gold and solid halos; strongly influenced by Gothic and Byzantine works
  • No true one or two point perspective, only approximations
  • Mostly paintings, few sculptures, if any
  • Architecture is short and human scale, round columns and arches

1450-1500

  • One point mathematical perspective
  • Many religious images, though other subjects become more common, such as portraits
  • More realistic figures
  • Begin to include patrons in the images

1500-1550

  • Very realistic figures and landscapes
  • Both religious subjects and non religious subjects; halos are small or gone in religious work
  • Both sculpture and painting, as well as prints
